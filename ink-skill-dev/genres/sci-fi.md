## Genre Prohibitions

- Tech rules changing to serve plot convenience — once physics/tech is established, it must stay consistent
- Technology solving everything — every tech must have limitations; introduce problems tech cannot fix (corruption, emotion, human greed)
- Info-dumping science/tech explanations outside of plot-critical moments (textbook paragraphs = reader exits)
- Ignoring logical consequences of technology — FTL, AI, biotech all have societal implications
- Hand-waving hard-science concepts in hard sci-fi without clear intent to treat science as soft
- Characters behaving as if from present day when story is set centuries ahead — cultural/linguistic adaptation matters
- "As you know, Captain" exposition between characters who both already know the fact
- Surprise capabilities unveiled at climax without prior foreshadowing
- Alien/AI characters whose psychology is just a human with costume

## Tech Consistency Rules

- Every technology must have defined limitations and side effects (cost, cooldown, failure mode, ethical tradeoff)
- New technologies create new problems — they don't just solve old ones
- If the story uses FTL, hyperdrives, or teleportation, establish rules in the first 3 chapters and stick to them
- **Hard sci-fi**: explain the science, make it plausible, build consequences. Readers will check. Math must survive a fact-check
- **Space opera**: science can be soft, but internal rules must be consistent across the narrative
- Show technology through character interaction, not textbook entries
- Era research required: reference real science correctly, extrapolate plausibly
- **Tech reveal budget**: major new tech reveal once per 5 chapters max; more and reader fatigues on worldbuilding

## Checkpoint Checklist (apply while writing)

| # | Check | Action if fails |
|---|-------|-----------------|
| 1 | Is this chapter's tech usage consistent with rules established in earlier chapters? | No → fix this chapter OR earlier rules, but do not hand-wave |
| 2 | Does any tech solve a problem without cost / limitation / tradeoff? | Yes → add cost (resource, time, ethical, political) |
| 3 | Is there an info-dump longer than 2 paragraphs? | Yes → split into character-driven reveals across scenes |
| 4 | Do non-human characters (alien / AI) show non-human thought patterns? | No → add 1 behavioral tell that signals alien cognition |
| 5 | Is the setting's timeline clear (year / century / relative to present)? | No → anchor via object / cultural reference / dated log |
| 6 | Is the political / factional landscape referenced in this chapter? | No for 4+ chapters → add faction signal even in combat chapter |
| 7 | Did a surprise capability appear without setup? | Yes → add earlier foreshadowing OR cut the surprise |
| 8 | Does dialogue avoid "as you know" exposition? | No → cut and reveal via action / consequence |

## Pacing Guidance

- Hard sci-fi: logical problem-solving drives pacing — each chapter should advance understanding or create new constraints
- Space opera: epic scale requires political/interpersonal arcs between action sequences
- Exploration chapters establish wonder and worldbuilding through character experience
- Political complexity: factions with competing interests, diplomacy alongside combat
- Tech reveals at plot-critical moments only — never dump specs for their own sake
- Action scenes grounded in established physics/tech rules — no surprise capabilities
- Settings spanning star systems need clear spatial orientation for readers (distance / travel time / cultural gap)
- Moral Reckoning chapters: every 8-12 chapters, force protagonist to confront the cost of a tech-enabled choice

## Language Discipline

- Show tech through consequence, not spec sheet. ✗"The gravitational anchor uses 4.2 petajoules per second" → ✓"The lights dimmed every seven seconds, matching the anchor's cycle"
- Alien / AI dialogue should deviate from human rhythm in at least one concrete way (sentence length, reference frame, missing / extra social lubricant)
- Avoid the "translator-perfect" convention unless the plot uses translation as a theme
- Jargon density: a reader with no prior sci-fi vocabulary should follow the chapter's plot without a glossary
- When a physics / biology / chemistry term appears, ground it in observable effect within the same paragraph

## Narrative Guidance

Worldbuilding emerges through character experience. The reader learns about the spaceship by watching someone fix it, not by reading a deck-plan.

Tech is a lens on human nature, not a replacement for it. The most interesting sci-fi problems are about people adapting to technology — who benefits, who is excluded, who resists.

Every faction / species has internal diversity. The Federation is not monolithic, the alien race has dissenters, the AI collective has outliers.

Action grounded in rules: if a character can teleport, establish it in chapter 1; if she suddenly teleports in chapter 20 without setup, the reader feels cheated.

Political subplots matter because technology creates / destroys power balances. Every major tech reveal should shift faction standing.
